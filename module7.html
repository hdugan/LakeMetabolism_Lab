Module 6 estimated GPP and ER from one single night. Now zoom all the way out: this is that same calculation, repeated automatically for every single day of a real open-water season — March through November 2024, the same Lake Mendota buoy. Slide through spring, summer, and fall and watch the lake's whole metabolic year unfold.

Module 6's night-slope / day-slope arithmetic has a name: bookkeeping. It's the simplest way to pull GPP and ER out of an oxygen curve, but it's not the only one. Below, switch between bookkeeping and four more advanced methods — all borrowed from the same statistical toolkit as the R package LakeMetabolizer — fit to the exact same buoy data.

The missing season

This chart starts in mid-March and ends in late November — not because nothing happens in winter, but because the buoy isn't there to see it. Every year, it comes out of the water before ice-in and goes back in after ice-out, the same as almost every lake-monitoring buoy in a climate like Wisconsin's. Winter (and under-ice) metabolism is real, and ecologically important, but measuring it requires different equipment entirely — it's a genuine blind spot, not a gap in this app.

What the data actually shows

Bookkeeping GPP/ER come from the same automated night-slope / day-slope method used in Module 6; OLS, MLE, Kalman filter, and Bayesian (Metropolis-Hastings MCMC) all fit the same alpha/R/gas-exchange model used in Modules 3 & 4 to a 48-hour window around each day instead. All five are run once per day across the full 2024 open-water season (EDI knb-lter-ntl.129). Every method is noisy day to day - a single sensor blip can throw off one day's number - so every line above shows a 7-day rolling median rather than the raw daily estimate.
