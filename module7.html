Seasonal Metabolism

Module 6 estimated GPP and ER from one single night. Now zoom all the way out: this is that same calculation, repeated automatically for every single day of a real open-water season — March through November 2024, the same Lake Mendota buoy. Slide through spring, summer, and fall and watch the lake's whole metabolic year unfold.

Module 6's day-slope/night-slope approach is called the bookkeeping method. It's the simplest way to estimate GPP and ER from an oxygen time series, making it an excellent tool for learning the basic concepts — but the by-hand version you used there skipped atmospheric gas exchange entirely. The bookkeeping method below is the real thing: LakeMetabolizer's own implementation, which subtracts an estimated lake-atmosphere oxygen flux — based on wind speed and how far the lake sits from oxygen saturation — from every hourly change before splitting day from night. That correction still needs a mixed-layer depth, which this buoy doesn't measure, so it's fixed at 2 meters all season here; reasonable for summer, but a likely overcorrection once the lake mixes deeper after fall turnover (details in the footer). Modern lake metabolism studies typically go a step further, fitting gas exchange directly from the data instead of assuming a fixed depth, and accounting for uncertainty day to day. Below, compare bookkeeping with four such methods, all implemented in the R package LakeMetabolizer, using the exact same buoy data.

The missing season

This chart starts in mid-March and ends in late November — not because nothing happens in winter, but because the buoy isn't there to see it. Every year, it comes out of the water before ice-in and goes back in after ice-out, the same as almost every lake-monitoring buoy in a climate like Wisconsin's. Winter (and under-ice) metabolism is real, and ecologically important, but measuring it requires different equipment entirely — it's a genuine blind spot, not a gap in this app.

Do autotrophic periods line up with algal blooms?

The buoy actually carries two pigment sensors, not one. Chlorophyll fluorescence tracks biomass from nearly any kind of algae. Phycocyanin fluorescence is specific to cyanobacteria — "blue-green algae," the kind behind most harmful algal bloom advisories. Comparing the two can reveal which algae are behind a given bloom, not just that one is happening.

What the data actually shows

Bookkeeping GPP/ER use LakeMetabolizer's own metab.bookkeep() (Cole et al. 2000): hourly night/day DO changes with an estimated atmospheric flux subtracted first, using k600 from wind speed (Cole & Caraco 1998) converted to a temperature-specific gas-exchange rate via the Schmidt number. That flux correction needs a mixed-layer depth, which this buoy doesn't measure, so it's fixed at 2 meters all season — a simplification that's reasonable for summer stratification but likely overstates the correction once the lake mixes far deeper than that after fall turnover, so bookkeeping's September–November numbers should be read with that caveat in mind. OLS, MLE, Kalman filter, and Bayesian (Metropolis-Hastings MCMC) all fit the same alpha/R/gas-exchange model used in Modules 3 & 4 to a 48-hour window around each day instead. All five are run once per day across the full 2024 open-water season (EDI knb-lter-ntl.129). Every method is noisy day to day - a single sensor blip can throw off one day's number - so every line above shows a 7-day rolling median rather than the raw daily estimate.
