The Oxygen Budget

Think of dissolved oxygen as moving between two connected pools: the lake and the atmosphere. Three "pipes" move oxygen into or out of the lake. Photosynthesis is one pipe that adds oxygen to the lake whenever there's enough light. Respiration is a second pipe that removes oxygen from the lake continuously, both day and night. The third pipe, gas exchange, connects the lake and the atmosphere. Oxygen can flow from the atmosphere into the lake when the lake is oxygen-poor, or from the lake into the atmosphere when the lake contains more oxygen than the water can hold. Together, these three processes make up the lake's oxygen budget.

Concentration vs. Solubility vs. Saturation

Dissolved oxygen can be described in three related ways.

  • Concentration (mg/L) is the actual amount of oxygen dissolved in one liter of water.
  • Solubility is the maximum amount of oxygen that water can hold at a given temperature.
  • Saturation (%) compares the current concentration to the water's solubility. A lake at 100% saturation contains exactly as much oxygen as the atmosphere would maintain at that temperature. Values above 100% are supersaturated, while values below 100% are undersaturated.

Because solubility changes with temperature, the same dissolved oxygen concentration (mg/L) can represent different saturation values in cold versus warm water. Likewise, two lakes at 100% saturation can have different oxygen concentrations if their temperatures are different.

Equilibrium (100% saturation) — the gray dashed line

Equilibrium is the oxygen level the lake would settle at if biology took a break and only the atmosphere were involved — 100% air saturation, set purely by water temperature. It's not a fixed number: the line drifts a little through the week as the water warms and cools.

So what does it mean when the blue oxygen line sits above the gray equilibrium line? The lake is supersaturated — it contains more oxygen than the atmosphere would maintain at that temperature. Gas exchange always acts to move the lake back toward equilibrium, so oxygen moves from the lake into the atmosphere until the excess is removed.

When the blue line is below the equilibrium line, the lake is undersaturated, and oxygen flows from the atmosphere into the lake. The farther the lake is from equilibrium, the stronger this exchange becomes.

Biology often dominates, but physics sets the baseline

Everything above included photosynthesis and respiration pushing oxygen around. But underneath all that biology, there's a simpler physical process always running in the background: gas exchange, which alone always pulls oxygen toward equilibrium — temperature sets the equilibrium target, wind sets the speed at which it reaches equilibrium.
