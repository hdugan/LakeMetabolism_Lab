Why is atmospheric exchange negative?

This lake starts the week well above equilibrium — supersaturated with oxygen, the way Module 3 described. Gas exchange always pushes oxygen toward equilibrium (the gray dashed line), never away from it. So when oxygen concentration is higher than equilibrium (supersaturated), gas exchange is pulling oxygen out of the lake and into the air.

That's what a negative number means here: the lake is losing oxygen to the atmosphere, not gaining. It's also why turning up your gas-exchange slider pulls your model curve down toward the gray line rather than up.

Your model is the same production–respiration–gas-exchange equation from Module 3, driven by the same week's real light, wind, and temperature data. "Best fit" is the combination of the three dials that comes closest to the real observed oxygen curve (EDI knb-lter-ntl.129, 130) — found the same way a computer would, by searching for the settings that minimize the gap between the two curves. Even the best fit doesn't match perfectly: real lakes are messier than any three-process model.
