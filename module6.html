Every Lake Has a Metabolism

You have a metabolism. When you eat, you gain energy. When you exercise, you burn it. Scientists can even estimate your metabolic rate just by measuring how quickly you consume oxygen. A lake is no different — except instead of one organism breathing, an entire ecosystem is breathing: every fish, every bacterium, every alga, every plant, all at once. This module gives that whole-lake metabolism its two most important numbers, then has you calculate them yourself from one real night.

An ecosystem, breathing

Every organism in the lake is quietly doing the same two things your body does: building itself up using energy from food (or, for algae and plants, from sunlight), and breaking itself down to release that energy again. Add up that activity across the whole lake, all at once, and you get one number for the whole ecosystem — its metabolism.

🐟🦠🌿🍃🦆🐌

Every fish. Every bacterium. Every alga. Every plant. All together, they create one ecosystem-wide metabolism.

Two big numbers

Scientists give this whole-ecosystem activity two names.

☀️GPP

Gross Primary Production. The total amount of oxygen produced by photosynthesis, from every alga and plant in the lake, added together.

🫧ER

Ecosystem Respiration. The total amount of oxygen consumed by every living thing in the lake — fish, bacteria, algae, plants — added together.

Ecosystem Respiration beat Gross Primary Production that day. The lake consumed more oxygen than it produced: it's heterotrophic. It's living off stored food — instead of producing enough new organic matter, organisms are consuming material that already exists: dead leaves, algae from an earlier bloom, or carbon washed in from the surrounding watershed.

Five days later, the balance flipped. Gross Primary Production beat Ecosystem Respiration: the lake is autotrophic. It's acting like a factory — it builds more organic material than it burns, and there's extra production left over.

The farm analogy

Think of a farm. A productive farm grows more food than its residents can eat, creating a surplus. A less productive farm can't feed itself and has to bring food in from elsewhere.

A lake has a similar "carbon economy." An autotrophic lake produces more organic matter than it consumes, building a surplus. A heterotrophic lake consumes more than it produces and must rely on organic matter imported from the surrounding landscape or stored from previous production — like living on imports or savings.
